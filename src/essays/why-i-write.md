A few months ago I watched something happen in the studio that would have taken a full team weeks to pull off — done in a fraction of the time, because an AI agent had stepped into part of the work we used to do by hand. That's what stopped me. Not the result itself, but the speed at which it arrived. A year earlier, that same conversation would have ended with "maybe someday." Now it ends with "let's try this week."

I've been running Veles Productions since 2022. We started in virtual production — LED walls, motion capture, real-time rendering in Unreal Engine — the kind of work that until recently needed whole teams of specialists and budgets only large productions could afford. Today that same set of skills is meeting AI agents, robotics, and tools that didn't exist a few years ago. The industry hasn't changed. What's possible inside it has — and it's moving at a pace that's hard to capture over coffee, and even harder to fit into a single LinkedIn post.

That's why this blog exists. I'm starting it because writing is, for me, a way of thinking, not just a way of communicating something I already know. When I sit down to describe a problem my team wrestled with, or a decision we made against our own assumptions from six months earlier, that's when I actually see what I think.

> The future of creativity is being built right now, and it's being built in practice — in studios where film, animation, and technology meet in the same workshop, not in abstract predictions about what will happen a decade from now.

I write to think out loud about what I'm learning every day running a company that sits exactly on that intersection. And I write to invite into that conversation the people standing next to me, or the ones just starting to move toward this same place.

You won't find forecasts here like "everything will be different in five years" without anything to back them up. I'd rather write about what I actually see up close: how a production team's work changes when an AI agent takes over part of it; where creativity gets stronger thanks to new tools, and where it loses something we don't have a name for yet; what it's like to build a technology company in Poland that wants to compete with studios from the West and from Silicon Valley. This won't be a motivational blog, or a ten-tips listicle. These will be essays — sometimes longer, sometimes shorter, but always written from the position of someone who, that same day, had his hands dirty with the exact problem he's writing about.

The rhythm will be steady: a new essay every two weeks, in Polish and in English, always illustrated — because an image sometimes says more than a paragraph, and I like thinking visually too. If you'd rather listen, every essay will also have an audio version. I'm not promising viral takes or easy answers. I'm promising honesty about what I don't know, and specifics about what I've actually learned.

I'm starting this blog with a bit of apprehension, because writing in public carries a different kind of weight than talking inside a team — you can't undo a sentence the way you can undo a command in a terminal. But maybe that's exactly the reason to start now, while I'm still learning the most. If you work on similar things — creativity, technology, building something at the intersection of the two — come in. I'll write the way I'd explain something to someone I respect: exactly what I'm trying to understand myself.
