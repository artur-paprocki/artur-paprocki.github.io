A perfect storm doesn't come from one enormous wave. It comes from two medium-sized waves that nobody would notice on their own, meeting in the same place at the same moment and stacking up so that each feeds its energy into the other. Something like that is happening right now, in front of my eyes, in short vertical dramas made for the phone. The first wave is cost: thanks to generative AI, producing an episode that recently required a crew, a set and weeks of work is getting roughly ten times cheaper. The second wave is the viewer, who has moved to a phone held upright, with an attention span stretched to exactly the length of a subway ride between two stations. When those two waves meet at once, we don't simply get cheaper film or shorter video. We get a new genre.

The scale of what's being born is best shown by numbers, not by my impressions. According to estimates from the research firm Omdia, global micro-drama revenues reached around 11 billion dollars in 2025, with roughly 83 percent still coming from China, and the forecast for 2026 already speaks of 14 billion. Industry estimates, cited by Deloitte among others, put the number of micro-drama viewers in China at 662 million, and by mid-2024 the format had already reached 52 percent of Chinese internet users. Media Partners Asia calculated that in 2025 the market generated 9.4 billion dollars in revenue — for the first time in history more than the country's box office. Deloitte's predictions for 2026 show how fast the other side of the same market is growing outside China: global in-app revenues from micro-series more than doubled, from about 3.8 billion dollars in 2025 to a forecast 7.8 billion in 2026, with the first quarter alone growing from 178 million dollars in early 2024 to about 700 million a year later. The United States accounted for roughly half of those global revenues in 2025, though Deloitte expects that share to fall to around 40 percent in 2026 as the rest of the world catches up. About 30 percent of American Gen Z and millennials already know the format. This is not a niche you can calmly watch from the sidelines. It is a wave that has already flooded one market and is looking for new shores.

In my previous essay I wrote about a Chinese director who traded the film set for an image generator and now needs three months of AI work to earn what one month of traditional production used to pay him. His story is interesting in itself, but here I'm after something broader than one man's craft: what this wave does to the quality of what gets made, once you multiply it by hundreds of thousands of such workshops at once.

The answer I have at hand doesn't come from the micro-drama business but from a study published in 2024 in Science Advances by Doshi and Hauser. The researchers gave some writers access to story ideas suggested by a language model and asked them to write short stories. The effect turned out to be double-edged. On one hand, the AI-assisted texts were judged more creative and better written than those without support — and the writers who had previously done worst gained the most. On the other hand, those same texts, set side by side, turned out to be more similar to one another than stories written without AI. The floor of creativity rose for each person individually. The ceiling of diversity dropped for everyone collectively. That is exactly the mechanism I see when I scroll through micro-dramas one after another — not because the vertical screen forces banality, but because a cheap tool, used without a voice of one's own, leads thousands of creators to very similar solutions at once. The format is not the culprit. The culprit is what people do with the tool when nobody asks them to look for something of their own.

When Alfred Hitchcock was building the grammar of modern cinema, nobody needed a story legible on a phone held upright, in the gap between one subway station and the next. And yet it is him I keep coming back to, because the craft he built came before any format and will outlast every one of them. In "Vertigo" from 1958, Hitchcock popularised a technique known today as the dolly zoom, or the Vertigo effect: the camera pulls back from the character while the lens simultaneously zooms in, changing focal length, so that the face stays exactly the same size in the frame while the entire world around it stretches and heaves. The viewer isn't told that the character is dizzy. The viewer feels it before understanding why. It's easy to forget this in conversations about AI and production costs, but it is proof that film technique alone can carry an emotion that exists in no script — if someone knows what they are using it for.

> Technology and the market are that camera move today. The story and the human being must stay the same size in the frame.

I treat that same frame as a description of what is happening around me in this industry. Technology is getting cheaper and faster. The market moves, grows, changes the rules of the game month to month. It is the same camera move — a pull-back and a zoom-in at once, both happening in the same instant. The question I keep asking myself is: what should stay at the centre of that frame, the same size, no matter how much everything else stretches around it. For me the answer is simple — the story and the human being. If I let the camera move swallow them too, all I'm left with is vertigo with no emotion underneath.

I write this as someone who happens to be working on micro-dramas right now, so let me say it plainly instead of hedging: I don't believe this format condemns a creator to revenge plots, secret billionaires and a twist every ten seconds, even though I know that is what most people who have heard of it at all associate it with today. The vertical screen and the short episode are just a vessel. You can pour into it a series with the quality we associate with premium production, a story a brand wants to tell in a way somebody will actually watch to the end, or educational material that teaches something in the space of one subway ride instead of a forty-minute lecture nobody finishes. Our goal at the studio is premium production in this format — real actors and real emotions on set, virtual sets instead of building scenery from scratch every time, and AI exactly where it genuinely multiplies possibilities: in locations, in backdrops, in language versions of the same scene for another market. I don't want AI deciding whether a take is good. I want it to generate ten variants of a backdrop I could never have afforded anyway.

I have been running Veles since 2022, so I watch this same mechanism from two sides at once — as a creator reaching for new tools, and as someone who has to decide where to draw the line before the market draws it for him. The Doshi and Hauser study doesn't tell me AI is bad for creativity. It tells me that creativity without a voice of its own converges, whatever tool we happen to be holding. Hitchcock had no AI and still knew how to make a frame you feel before you understand it. I have AI, and I want to use it exactly where it lets me make more frames like that — not where it lets me make more of them per hour. I don't yet know whether micro-dramas will turn out to be a format where that line can be held for the long run, or whether the wave of economics will eventually flood it too. I only know that I would rather try to keep the story and the human being the same size in the frame than decide in advance that in this format it isn't even worth trying.
